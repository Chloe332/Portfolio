TYRE DEGRADATION
How lap time increases as tyres wear out
What is tyre degradation? As a tyre wears down, its rubber grip decreases and lap times get slower. The degradation rate (sec/lap) tells you exactly how much time is lost per lap due to wear. A rate of +0.05 s/lap means after 20 laps the driver is ~1 second slower from tyre wear alone. Pit stops, safety car laps, and statistical outliers are removed before calculating. The fitted line uses robust regression (Huber method) which ignores unusual laps.

RACE PACE
Lap-by-lap time comparison across drivers
How to read race pace: Each line = one driver's lap times through the race. Drops between stints = fresh tyre gain after a pit stop. Rising lines = tyre degradation setting in. A flat line = good tyre management (driver saving tyres). A sudden spike = safety car, traffic, or driver error. Compare when each driver's line starts climbing to see who ran out of tyre first — this reveals who got their strategy right.

WEATHER CONDITIONS
Track & air temperature affect tyre behaviour fundamentally
Why track temperature matters: F1 tyres have an ideal operating window (~80–110°C rubber surface). When track surface temperature rises above ~45°C, the tyre overheats, the rubber degrades faster, and lap times fall off a cliff. Teams factor this into strategy — in Bahrain's 50°C track, softs might only last 12 laps. The same compound in Monaco at 28°C might last 30 laps. Rain changes everything: slick tyres lose grip entirely and the car becomes undriveable — teams must switch to intermediate or full wet tyres instantly.
